How Powerful is Your Passport?

Using the Henley Passport Index to illustrate the travel freedom of ordinary (non-diplomatic) passports around the world.

Travel has become more accessible over time


In 2008, very few regions such as the United States of America, parts of Europe and Australia were able to fly freely to over 150 countries across the world. As of today, more countries have increased their travel freedom including countries in Asia and South America.


Compared to the past where most countries had been limited in their passport strength, only a handful of countries are restricted to less than 50 visa-free travel locations.



Some continents such as Oceania do not have enough data to make observations about patterns. However there are few noteworthy points to be made;

Taking a look at the passport index data obtained in 2022, we can note that the the African continent skews right, with most countries in the content being able to access less than 80 countries.

In contrast, we can note that most European countries have very high travel freedom. As noted by the graph, other than a few countries like Belarus and Serbia, most countries in Europe can travel to more than 150 countries without a visa.

North America has a relatively even distribution of travel freedom, with the largest portion being able to travel to 140 to 160 countries.






The top 10 countries with the most powerful passports have changed significantly over the years.


The current highest ranked country, Singapore entered the top 10 in 2014 and steadily climbed up the rankings placing first in 3 non-consecutive years.


The United States of America that started off at the top spot in 2006 has had drastic fluctuations, dropping to the 10th position in 2011, rising back to number 1 in 2014, and finally altogether dropping out of the top 10 rankings in 2019.




For some continents, the GDP of a country appear to have a relationship with the number of countries it can travel to without a visa.


This holds true for Asian countries such as United Arab Emirates, and appears to be a common trend with countries have higher GDP having greater travel freedom.


An exception to this is the African continent, where the number of countries allowing visa-free entry appear to fall within a similar range irrespective of a country's GDP.
